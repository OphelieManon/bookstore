Lessons in Chemistry

by Bonnie Garmus

Paperback: 400 pages

Language: English

Genre: Best sellers,Mothers & Children Fiction,Humorous Fiction

Book Detail

Chemist Elizabeth Zott is not your average woman. In fact, Elizabeth Zott would be the first to point out that there is no such thing as an average woman. But it’s the early 1960s and her all-male team at Hastings Research Institute takes a very unscientific view of equality. Except for one: Calvin Evans; the lonely, brilliant, Nobel–prize nominated grudge-holder who falls in love with—of all things—her mind. True chemistry results.
But like science, life is unpredictable. Which is why a few years later Elizabeth Zott finds herself not only a single mother, but the reluctant star of America’s most beloved cooking show Supper at Six. Elizabeth’s unusual approach to cooking (“combine one tablespoon acetic acid with a pinch of sodium chloride”) proves revolutionary. But as her following grows, not everyone is happy. Because as it turns out, Elizabeth Zott isn’t just teaching women to cook. She’s daring them to change the status quo.

Review

By Bibliophile, April 19, 2023

This book was a refreshing tale of fiction, or was it? Elizabeth Zott was a brilliant scientist in the 1960s. This was a time when women were told to stay in the kitchen. Yet, the suppression of women hasn't ended, really. As a woman myself, I went through many of the same types of things this character did. So many women have. This is from being taken less seriously than a man to being passed over for promotions so a man could take the reigns.
The story takes many surprising turns, which makes it a natural page turner. The dog in the story is integrated as a fascinating character in its own right. Many of the unbelievable aspects of the main character are actually drawn from real life. Societal norms are not real but forced upon intelligent individuals. Society forces segments of the population into a corner in an attempt to keep them from succeeding. Many men may not relate to this tale, but women surely can. I recommend this book for all the women or anyone for that matter whose sights, they are told, exceed their grasp. This is for those individuals who need to listen to their heart and mind instead of those of the oppressors.
